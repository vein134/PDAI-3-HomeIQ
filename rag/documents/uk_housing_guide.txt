TITLE: UK First-Time Buyer Guide 2024-2025

SECTION: Help to Buy and Government Schemes
The UK government offers several schemes to help first-time buyers get on the property ladder. The Lifetime ISA (LISA) allows savers aged 18-39 to save up to £4,000 per year and receive a 25% government bonus (up to £1,000/year). Funds can be used for a first home worth up to £450,000. The Shared Ownership scheme lets buyers purchase a share (25-75%) of a property and pay rent on the remaining share. Right to Buy allows council tenants in England to buy their home at a discount. First Homes is a scheme offering new-build homes to first-time buyers at a discount of at least 30%.

SECTION: Mortgage Types Explained
Fixed-rate mortgages lock in your interest rate for a set period (typically 2 or 5 years). During this period, your monthly payments stay the same regardless of Bank of England base rate changes. After the fixed period ends, you move to the lender's Standard Variable Rate (SVR), which is usually higher. Tracker mortgages follow the Bank of England base rate plus a set percentage. They can be cheaper initially but carry the risk of rate increases. Offset mortgages link your savings to your mortgage, so you only pay interest on the difference between your mortgage balance and your savings.

SECTION: Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT)
As of 2024-2025, the SDLT thresholds are: 0% on the first £250,000 (£425,000 for first-time buyers on properties up to £625,000). 5% on the portion from £250,001 to £925,000. 10% on the portion from £925,001 to £1,500,000. 12% on anything above £1,500,000. Additional property surcharge is 3% on top of each band. Non-UK residents pay an additional 2% surcharge.

SECTION: The Buying Process
Step 1: Get a mortgage agreement in principle (AIP). Step 2: Find a property and make an offer. Step 3: Once accepted, instruct a solicitor/conveyancer. Step 4: Arrange a survey (basic valuation, homebuyer report, or full building survey). Step 5: Your solicitor conducts searches (local authority, environmental, drainage). Step 6: Exchange contracts with a deposit (usually 10%). Step 7: Complete the purchase and collect keys. The process typically takes 8-12 weeks from offer acceptance to completion.

SECTION: Costs Beyond the Deposit
Solicitor/conveyancer fees: £800-£2,000. Survey costs: £250-£1,500 depending on type. Mortgage arrangement fees: £0-£2,000. Valuation fee: £150-£1,500. Removal costs: £500-£2,000. Buildings insurance: required from exchange of contracts. Furnishing costs: budget £2,000-£10,000+. Energy Performance Certificate: ~£60-£120.

SECTION: Rent vs Buy Decision Framework
Key factors to consider: How long do you plan to stay? (Buying typically becomes more cost-effective after 5+ years.) What are current mortgage rates vs rental yields? What is your savings position and income stability? Are property prices expected to rise or fall in your area? Consider opportunity cost — if deposit money were invested in the stock market, historical returns average 7-10% annually. Factor in maintenance costs for homeownership (typically 1-2% of property value per year). Consider the flexibility premium of renting — no negative equity risk, easier to relocate.

SECTION: Understanding Affordability
Most UK lenders will offer 4-4.5x your annual household income. Some specialist lenders offer up to 5.5x for certain professions (doctors, accountants, lawyers). Your monthly mortgage payment should ideally be no more than 28-35% of your gross monthly income. Stress testing: lenders check you can afford payments if rates rise by 3 percentage points. Debt-to-income ratio matters — existing loans, car finance, and credit card debt reduce borrowing capacity.
